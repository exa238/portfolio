Growthbot

About the Project

I created an interactive botanical diorama inspired by my love for plants and botany. My goal was to simulate plant growth using physical components, motion, and light, while blending craft, electronics, and creative coding into a single immersive experience.

This project evolved through two major iterations. The first focused on motion and physical construction; the second expanded into lighting effects, ambient sound, and improved aesthetics.

Project Goals

  • Build a growth simulation using servos
  • Design and fabricate custom plant leaves
  • Create a visually appealing miniature garden
  • Explore light and sound to enhance immersion
  • Iterate based on challenges and discoveries

My Role

  • Designer
  • Fabrication
  • Prototyping
  • Programming

Design and Fabrication

Planning

I first created a concept sketch that documentented each piece I wanted to include and how I wanted them to move basically.

Leaf Design

I couldn’t find SVGs that matched the leaf shapes I wanted, so I drew them in Procreate. I I exported them and converted them to SVG using an online converter, and cut them using a Cricut, ultimately using two leaf designs in the final build.

Building the Diorama

I recycled an old blanket box as the base and convered the interior with construction paper. I used hot glue to glue the moss to the background and floor an created the plants' stems using lollipop sticks wrapped in green tape. Finally, I mounted servos to each of the stems and attched leaves to the servos, making sure to adjust the angles and heights with repeated testing. I then added battery-operated fairy lights to the mossy background for a more aesthetically pleasing look.

Programming

I initially tried assigning separate variables for each servo. Eventually, I discovered all three could run smoothly without individual variable declarations. This simplified the code and reduced unnecessary complexity.

Challenges and Tradeoffs

  • The box was too narrow, preventing me from adding a third leaf and servo
  • My hot glue gun broke mid-build, forcing me to use tape.
  • I wanted programmable LEDs instead of battery-operated lights
  • In my initial sketch and concept, I designed a servo-powered moving sign, but lacked time, space and adhesive strength
  • I wanted to experiment with ambient sound and incorporate bird calls using wav files

Revision

Lighting Improvements

After presenting the first version, I revisited the project to improve the atmosphere, electronics, and overall presentation.

I then shifted to a blue and white hue and added periodic irregular flashes. This created a much more convincing lightning effect.

Sound Exploration

I downloaded 16- bit, mono, 22,050 hz WAV files from freesound.org, and despite correct formatting, the files still wouldn't play

I tried incorporating manual chirp sounds programmatically, however this aso fauled due to hardware limitations

As a final sound solution, I played rainforest ambience from my computer during the demo

Wiring Improvements

I connected the servos to A1 and connected the LED panel via a separate male alligator clip to A2, and ensured proper grounding and breadboard routing, resolving earlier power conflicts.

Decorative Improvements

I added flowers to the background, positioning ferns and other botanicals for depth. I also used hot glue to eliminate any visible tape and mounted the LED panel to the front interior wall for full-scene illumination

Before the final assembly, I ran a placement test to ensure the LED panel illuminated the entire diorama effectively.

Final Updated Project

The final version included:

  • battery-operated ambient lights
  • LED lightning simulation
  • Upgraded foliage and moss
  • Ambient rainforest audio (external)
  • Cleaner, more polished visual design
